Digital Power & Influence Index

A neutral, non-partisan framework for assessing modern influence across attention, capital, institutions, and networks. This site does not represent any official organisation, campaign, or public figure. It is an independent analytical project.

Framework overview

Influence today is not one dimension. It is a portfolio of capabilities that convert attention into outcomes: fundraising, policy leverage, platform reach, narrative dominance, and coalition control. This page defines a transparent rubric that can be applied to any high-profile actor (political, financial, cultural, or institutional).

Attention
A
Reach, salience, virality
Capital

Liquidity, fundraising, deal flow

What this is
A structured way to compare influence across actors and time, using explicit assumptions and update cadence. The objective is interpretability, not prediction certainty.
What this is not
Not a login portal, not a membership gate, not official, not affiliated with any political entity, and not providing financial advice.

How the index is used

  • Baseline: establish an initial score for each dimension.
  • Events: apply changes based on observable catalysts (platform bans, legal risk, funding events, coalition shifts).
  • Validation: compare against measurable proxies (polling deltas, fundraising filings, audience metrics, institutional outcomes).
  • Notes: record assumptions, evidence links, and uncertainty.

Methodology

The index is designed to be transparent and auditable. Every score should be explainable with references to observable indicators, and every update should include a short rationale. When evidence conflicts, the index records uncertainty rather than forcing precision.

Scoring rubric (0–100)

Band Interpretation Typical indicators
0–25 Low influence / fragmented capability Limited reach, weak funding, minimal institutional access, thin networks
26–50 Moderate, episodic influence Event-driven spikes, some sponsorship, partial access, inconsistent mobilisation
51–75 High influence with constraints Sustained attention, credible funding, meaningful access, strong coalition capacity
76–100 Dominant influence Agenda-setting reach, deep capital, durable institutional control, resilient networks

Evidence & uncertainty

Each dimension can be supported by publicly observable signals. Suggested evidence types include:
  • Attention: search interest, audience reach, media share, platform engagement
  • Capital: filings, fundraising reports, disclosed sponsorship, market proxies
  • Institutions: legal constraints, eligibility, formal roles, enforcement outcomes
  • Networks: endorsements, organisational capacity, distribution channels

Risk register (why influence changes)

Influence can swing quickly because modern systems are tightly coupled: platform rules, legal decisions, liquidity shocks, coalition fractures, and information cascades. The table below is a starter “risk register” used to annotate score changes.

Risk type Mechanism Impact on index Notes
Platform constraint Reach reduced via moderation, de-boosting, or account loss Attention ↓, Networks ↓ Often rapid; sometimes reversible
Legal / regulatory Eligibility, liability, enforcement, or injunction risk Institutions ↓, Capital ↓ Lagged but durable
Liquidity / funding Capital inflows/outflows, donor fatigue, sponsor flight Capital ↑/↓ Sensitive to narrative shocks
Coalition fracture Internal conflict, endorsement loss, organisational breakdown Networks ↓, Institutions ↓ Often visible via public defections
Information cascade Rapid belief shift driven by viral story cycles Attention ↑/↓ (volatile) High noise; track primary sources
